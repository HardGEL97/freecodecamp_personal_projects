History and Background

The Roland TB-303 was originally designed as a bass accompaniment for solo musicians, released in 1981.

However, it was largely a commercial failure until it was rediscovered in the mid-1980s by electronic musicians.

The unique "acid" sound of the TB-303 was created accidentally, and it defined the acid house genre.

Despite being discontinued, the TB-303 remains influential today, with many software and hardware clones.

The resurgence of this machine led to its use in many subgenres of electronic music, including techno, trance, and more.

  • Released in 1981
  • Originally designed for bass accompaniment
  • Failed commercially but succeeded later in electronic music

Physical Components and Layout

The TB-303 features a simple interface with knobs and switches used to shape the sound.

The most important controls are the tuning, cutoff, resonance, envelope mod, and decay knobs.

The switches allow users to select between sawtooth and square waveforms, as well as adjust patterns.

  • Tuning control for pitch adjustment
  • Cutoff and resonance to control the filter
  • Switches for waveform selection (sawtooth/square)

Sound Creation

The TB-303 uses a single oscillator that produces either a sawtooth or square wave.

The filter is essential in shaping the sound by altering the harmonic content using the cutoff and resonance controls.

Accent and slide features make it expressive, adding punch and note transitions.

  • Sawtooth and square wave options
  • Low-pass filter for sound shaping
  • Accent and slide for dynamic sound effects

Sequencer and Pattern Programming

The TB-303 has a step sequencer that allows users to program bass lines note by note.

Patterns are created by adjusting pitch, duration, accents, and slide between notes.

This step-by-step process enables users to create unique and repetitive bass lines, which define the acid house sound.

  • Step-based note entry
  • Accents for volume and tone changes
  • Slide for smooth note transitions
